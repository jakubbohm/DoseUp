THE VERSION-RANGE FINDING — why this GO has an expiry date.

The issue assumed EFCore.NamingConventions declares a dependency FLOOR (">= 10.0.0").
It does not. It declares a BOUNDED RANGE with an exclusive upper bound:

    Microsoft.EntityFrameworkCore            [10.0.1, 11.0.0)
    Microsoft.EntityFrameworkCore.Relational [10.0.1, 11.0.0)

Today that resolves silently and correctly, because NuGet/SemVer 2.0 sorts a prerelease
BELOW its release: 11.0.0-preview.5 < 11.0.0, so the previews satisfy "< 11.0.0".
That is why our restore is clean, with no NU1608, and why the package works at all.

The day EF Core 11.0.0 STABLE ships, that stops being true: 11.0.0 is NOT < 11.0.0.
NuGet then raises NU1608. Proof below, using the SAME range shape one major earlier
(NamingConventions 9.0.0, which bounds EF to [9.0.0, 10.0.0), against stable EF 10.0.1):

CONSEQUENCE FOR DoseUp: the repo root Directory.Build.props sets TreatWarningsAsErrors=true,
so this lands as a HARD RESTORE FAILURE in src/, not a warning — on the day we bump EF to
11.0.0 stable, which is a bump we control and will be doing deliberately.

This does not change the verdict. It makes the fallback trigger precise and impossible to
miss: if EFCore.NamingConventions has not shipped an 11.x by our EF-stable bump, either
NoWarn NU1608 after checking the release notes, or spend the ~20 lines on the hand-rolled
IModelFinalizingConvention. The naming decision is unaffected either way.
